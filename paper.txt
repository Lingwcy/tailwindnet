Abstract
Early identification of invasive plants such as Solidago canadensis requires separating species that are visually
similar in RGB imagery but physiologically distinguishable in near-infrared (NIR) bands. This makes RGB‚ÄìNIR
fusion appealing, yet also fragile: in our experiments, representative fusion strategies often underperform strong
single-modality baselines on the most spectrally confusable vegetation classes. We attribute this failure to feature
suppression, where premature feature blending attenuates modality-specific spectral signatures before they become
semantically organized. We propose Tailwind, a dual-branch RGB‚ÄìNIR segmentation network designed to fuse
only where fusion is beneficial. At shallow stages, a Gated Cross-Modal Fusion and Alignment (GCMFA) module
performs bidirectional cross-attention followed by spatially adaptive gated write-back, enabling selective exchange
while retaining modality-specific discriminability. At deep stages, a Multi-Depthwise Convolution Head Transposed
Attention (MDTA) module treats channels as spectral‚Äìsemantic tokens and reorganizes their correlations to emÔøæphasize invasive-target responses. A soft alignment loss stabilizes branch interaction without forcing hard feature
collapse. On our Multispectral Plant Scenarios (MPS) dataset, Tailwind achieves 87.06% mIoU and 80.12% IoU
for S. canadensis, improving over the strongest compared baseline by 5.32 percentage points. Results on MFNet and
detection transfer further indicate that selective fusion is a practical alternative to fuse-first multimodal pipelines.

3.1. Dataset and Settings
We construct the Multispectral Plant Scenarios (MPS)

the vegetative stage. This setting is deliberately difficult:
goldenrod lacks its later-season yellow flowers, several vegÔøæetation categories share similar RGB canopy texture, and
UAV scenes include illumination variation, dense overlap,
and mixed ground cover. Pixel-level masks were initialÔøæized with SAM [KMR‚àó
23] and manually reviewed by doÔøæmain experts; annotation consistency on a reviewed subÔøæset yields Cohen‚Äôs Œ∫=0.91. We merge 13 raw long-tail catÔøæegories into six semantic classes according to ecological relÔøæevance and sample frequency. The split is 2,280/390/590

rather than as the primary target domain. Training uses

3.2. Quantitative Results
Tailwind-B2 achieves 87.06% mIoU at 40k iterations and
89.68% at 80k on MPS, outperforming all baselines (TaÔøæble 1, Figure 4). The improvement is concentrated on the
classes that motivate RGB‚ÄìNIR fusion: Canadian goldenrod
improves by 7.50 pp over SegFormer-B0 and reed improves
by 12.80 pp, indicating that Tailwind exploits NIR cues
where RGB appearance alone is ambiguous. In contrast, sevÔøæeral generic fusion baselines underperform the single-branch
SegFormer-B0 on these vegetation classes (e.g., SeAFusion:
55.90% CG IoU), supporting the feature-suppression diagnoÔøæsis. All methods improve under 80k training without changÔøæing the ranking, suggesting that Tailwind‚Äôs advantage is not
simply a convergence-speed effect.

4. Discussion
The results support the proposed design rationale from
three complementary angles. First, degradation in earlyÔøæfusion baselines is class-selective: it is strongest for CanaÔøædian goldenrod and reed, where RGB appearance is amÔøæbiguous and NIR cues are expected to matter. Second, the
ablation study shows that selective shallow exchange is the
dominant contributor, consistent with the need to preserve
modality-specific cues before semantic abstraction. Third,
the channel‚Äìspectral heatmaps indicate that deep transÔøæposed attention increases the concentration of NIR-band reÔøæsponses, explaining why MDTA complements GCMFA. We
therefore use feature suppression as an empirical characteriÔøæzation of premature fusion rather than as a directly observed
physical process.
